CRISPR

Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats

The science of CRISPR

CRISPR was initially a mechanism found in bacteria that defended against invasions. It would destroy the DNA of the intruder, killing it. This mechanism can be used for gene editing: cutting DNA at specific sites and modifying it. In gene editing, CRISPR consists of the protein, usually Cas9, and guide RNA. When the gRNA binds to Cas9, the protein is activated, and searches the target DNA for the binding site. If a PAM sequence (series of three nucleotides) matches, the gRNA will pair with the DNA, and the DNA will be cut. After the cut, the cell will naturally try to repair the DNA. Either it will bind the two cut ends, resulting in a deletion, or fill it with another nucleotide series. By artificially adding the desired DNA into the sample, scientists can control the specific gene. Ultimately, CRISPR manipulates a cell into replacing a DNA sequence by cutting the DNA at a specific location.

The history of CRISPR

1987 First Citing of CRISPR
1993 Discovery of CRISPR
2005 Hypothetical Adaptive Immune System
2007 Confirmed Adaptive Immune System
2008 First Artificial CRISPR Arrays
2008 CRISPR targets DNA
2010 Cas9 cleaves target DNA
2011 CRISPR functions heterologously
2012 Using CRISPR In-Vitro
2013 CRISPR in Mammalian Cells
2018 First CRISPR Babies

Yoshizumi Ishino, Yale University
Ishino sequenced the iap gene in E. coli while studying the isozyme conversion of alkaline phosphatase. Ishino realized there were many repeat sequences in this gene. This sequence was repeated 5 times with a constant spacing between them. It turned out that Ishino was the first to find and cite a CRISPR sequence.

Francisco Mojica, University of Alicante
Mojica studied H. mediterranei, an archaeal microbe. Mojica sequenced a section of DNA and found multiple almost-palindromic repeated sequences of 30 bases that were separated by 36 base spacers. He realized similar structures were found in other distant microbes. Mojica published his findings on this new class of repetition, which would soon be coined CRISPR.

Francisco Mojica, University of Alicante
Mojica continued his research into these intriguing sequences. He tried to put the spacers between the CRISPR sequences into a BLAST to find similarities. Luckily, a spacer sequence of an E. coli strain matched that of a P1 phage which commonly infected E. coli. The particular E. coli strain was also immune to the P1 infection. Thus, Mojica realized these sequences were used as an adaptive immune system to protect microbe against infections.

Philippe Horvath, Danisco France SAS
Horvath studied how S. thermophilus, a bacteria used in dairy fermentation, fought off phage infections. He sought to test of Mojica's hypothesis with these bacteria. In his experiment, he isolated phage-resistant bacteria through genetic selection. The resistant strains were found to have acquired phage sequences in their CRISPR loci. Horvath also found that more spaces correlated to increased resistance. Finally, he realized that Cas9 was needed for resistance.

John van der Oost, University of Wageningen
Van der Oost characterized the Cas9 complexes surrounding CRISPR to get a better understanding on how CRISPR actually works. He found that spacer sequences were transcribed into RNA (coined crRNA), which leads the Cas9 proteins to cut DNA. His team then programmed the first artificial CRISPR array to target the lambda phage. As expected, the strains carrying new CRISPR array were immune to the lambda phage.

Luciano Marraffini and Erik Sontheimer, Northwestern University
Marraffini thought that CRISPR targeted DNA instead of RNA, which was a prevalent theory at the time. Marraffini and Sontheimer performed an experiment on the S. epidermidis bacteria using molecular biology techniques. Their experiment ultimately showed that CRISPR targeted DNA instead of RNA. They realized that CRISPR was a programmable restriction enzyme and could be used in genetic editing.

Sylvain Moineau, University of Laval
Previously, CRISPR worked too effectively to the point that it was hard to study how DNA was actually destroyed. In 2010, Moineau observed S. thermophilus, which only had partial protection through CRISPR. This allowed Moineau to study how CRISPR actually worked. They found that the cutting relied on only the Cas9 nuclease. They proved that the Cas9 nuclease cut DNA at precise locations defined by crRNA.

Virginijus Siksnys, Vilnius University
Siksnys wanted to see if it was possible to use a CRISPR system from one organism and use it in another. He extracted the CRISPR system from S. thermophilus and moved the whole CRISPR locus into E. coli; he found that this caused targeted interference at plasmids and phages by the E. coli. Siksnys found that Cas9 was the only nucleus needed for interference. At this point, all parts of CRISPR needed for interference were known and could now be applied to other experiments.

Virginijus Siksnys, Vilnius University
Siksnys continued his studies with CRISPR. His team purified the CRISPR system from S. thermophilus, and he showed that it could cut DNA at a precise target in vitro. More importantly, he also showed that they could reprogram Cas9 to cut DNA at a site he wanted in vitro. This lead to the realization that CRISPR could be used in many biotechnological ways.

Feng Zhang, Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard
Zhang made a three component system that could mutate genes in mice and humans with high efficiency and accuracy. He showed that the system could target multiple gene loci in human and mouse genes. He also showed that this system could make repairs based off homology. He would later show that CRISPR could be used to create complex models of inherited diseases in mice to find essential genes.

He Jiankui, Southern University of Science and Technology
He surprised the whole world when he claimed he helped create the first CRISPR babies. He claimed he edited the CCR5 gene using CRISPR in order to give the two twin girls immunity to HIV; he said the girls were born healthy. This event sparked outrage from the scientific community because of ethical and health concerns. He was sentenced to 3 years in prison and fined around $430,000 for unethical conduct.

How is CRISPR used?

Choose the gene

The first step is to choose the specific segment of DNA to modify. Many genes can be found in databases online, such as UniProt. The enzyme should also be considered, with Cas9 and Cpf1 being the most common types.

Make the gRNA

There are many programs that help with gRNA design (CRISPR Design, Benchling, GenScript, etcetera). The gRNA can be ordered through a DNA manufactures, who create synthetic DNA efficiently.

Assembly

We will use E. Coli as an example.

Grow the E. Coli in a petri dish with an arabinose mixture which contains repair proteins.

Insert 500 microliters of CaCl solution into a microcentrifuge tub via pipette. Make sure to keep tubes on ice when not using them.

Use a toothpick/loop to insert E. Coli to the tube.

Suspend the cells in a solution.

Add an expression vector via micropipette into the cells.

Insert the gRNA into the solution via micropipette.

Heat the tube up, then put the solution onto a plate.

Incubate the colonies (heat up for one day at 37C).

Ethics for CRISPR

Nontarget Mutations

CRISPR could lead to nontarget mutations especially when used in an embryo. These nontarget mutations could have a significant impact on a babies' lives and possibly the future generations of humans. Furthermore, it is difficult to track which mutations CRISPR caused in humans especially if the CRISPR babies are not constantly tracked. It will take years to be able to see nontarget mutations, and by that time, the lives of the first CRISPR babies could be at danger.

Ecological imbalance

It is hard to predict how edited genes will be passed down to future generations. Genes could be transferred from one species to the environment. Humans could create an imbalance in the ecosystem, which could have horrendous consequences. Furthermore, it will become challenging to control the use of CRISPR once it opens up, and thus the ecological impacts could nearly be unrestricted and irreversible.

Human Enhancement

Perhaps the scariest ethical consequence of CRISPR is the possibility of human enhancement. CRISPR could allow humans to alter their babies to make them more attractive, intelligent, or athletic. The possibility of programing a human corrupts one of the building blocks of evolution and humanity: randomness. In addition, this technology would cause wealthier families to have an advantage in producing better offspring since they have the access to advanced technology, expanding the wealth and social gap among humans.

Consent

Since CRISPR might be used on babies, the ethical issue of consent comes to play. Is it fair to change a baby's genetics without their knowledge at all? What if there are consequential impacts on that baby's health? CRISPR will cause many babies to become science experiments rather than actual humans living a normal life because of how they will be monitored to see CRISPR's success.

Identity of Humanity

The use of CRISPR will arouse many philosophical and ethical questions. Who are we as a race? Should we reprogram ourselves to be smarter or better? Who is to say that we are actually humans if we are defining what the next generation of "humans" are? A lot of questions will be asked in defining who we want ourselves to be before CRISPR is to be fully developed and used.

Success

Another important question to ask before using this technology is what defines success. There must be a standard in defining the difference between a therapeutic use of CRISPR and an enhancement. The possibilities of this technology are endless in theory, but where should humans draw the line?

CRISPR's Applications

Climate Change

Biofuels produced by algae can serve as a substitute for fossil fuels. However, this is currently neither cost-efficient nor effective. CRISPR can be used to genetically modify algae and other microorganisms to produced biofuels at a far faster rate. Since CRISPR isn't very expensive, this would make the overall process more cost-productive, creating a viable and crucial energy source for the future.

Food

CRISPR can genetically modify crops/animals so that they can survive and last longer. This will help with world food shortages, as farmers will be able to produce more food, with less of it going to waste. CRISPR is especially useful with climate change threatening food production with more droughts and floods.

CRISPR would be more accurate than traditional GMOs, since it takes less time to develop and is more cost effective. Normal GMOs also introduce artificial genotypes into plants, which may have unintended consequences, whereas CRISPR-inserted DNA is carefully designed from known mutations.

Here are a few examples:

White mushrooms can brown slower and thus have a longer shelf life.

Oranges can resist citrus greening disease, which put the American orange industry at risk by quartering commerical orange production.

The chocolate industry is declining in part due to cocoa being succeptible to diseasea problem that can be solved with CRISPR.

CRISPR can help cows become more resistant against diseases like tuberculosis and better able to live in a diverse range of environments, which is very helpful when in the midst of climate change.

Human Diseases

Tests have already been conducted to treat certain types of diseases in humans. Many scientists support using CRISPR because it allows them to modify the DNA which specifically causes the disease. Genetic changes will not be passed onto future generations unless it's an edit to the sperm, egg, or embryo. This means that even if the treatment fails, future generations do not suffer any impacts.

There is already federal approval for CRISPR treatment for spinal muscular atrophy and blood cancers. While people usually associate CRISPR in humans with designer babies, there are serious ethical issues with that. Scientists currently prioritize disease treatment applications of CRISPR over vanity applications.

CRISPR can potentially treat conditions such as:

Sickle Cell Anemia

The disease is caused by blood cell mutations. Some people have a mutation that allows them to produce hemoglobin into adulthood. Using CRISPR, doctors could allow patients with sickle cell anemia to produce healthy blood cells. The treatment is mostly successful with no known side effects.

Blindness

Doctors are treating LCA10 with CRISPR. The blindness is caused by a single point mutation, and CRISPR is used to insert a functional mutation. There is no other treatment for this, and researchers are currently running trials for the treatment. Eyes are generally low-risk since changing the DNA there should not affect other locations.
